# Voice Profile — The Care Ranch

## Brand
- Name: The Care Ranch
- What we do: An immersive six-day leadership experience in the high Sonoran Desert of Arizona, grounded in neuroscience and embodied intelligence, that helps senior leaders restore nervous-system regulation, clean vision, and congruent leadership.
- Audience: Executives, senior leaders, and individuals who sense that something in their leadership or life is ready for recalibration, that there is a deeper layer to how they decide and lead, that they are ready to evolve into a new phase of development, and who want to reconnect with direction, clarity, and themselves. Secondary audience: HR leaders and organizations investing in senior talent, and (via the non-profit branch) young people without access to this kind of work.
- Language: English, translated and composed from a Dutch original. The translation reads as considered, slightly formal, non-idiomatic English. Keep it that way; don't colloquialize.

## Tone
- Grounded before poetic. Every feeling-led line is anchored by a physical or procedural detail. "The land is open. The rhythm is slow. The silence is your peace." sits next to "8 to 12 participants" and "USD 5,250 per person." Both voices coexist without clashing.
- Quiet authority, never salesy. No hype words, no superlatives, no "transform your life." The brochure names outcomes precisely ("Leaders return more coherent, not merely rested") and lets that do the work.
- Clinical-meets-contemplative. Neuroscience language ("neuromuscular pathways," "nervous system regulation," "congruence") is used alongside desert-poetic language ("the rhythm is slow," "vision becomes visible again") without apology or softening. The reader is treated as capable of holding both.

## Vocabulary Patterns
- Reach-for words: alignment, congruence, regulation, nervous system, rhythm, integration, clarity, perception, embodied, immersion, recalibration, grounded, presence, direction, vision, precision, reflection, patterns
- Reach-for phrases: "clean vision," "not X, but Y" (e.g. "not performed, but embodied"), "held on both sides by," "becomes visible," "restore congruence," "under pressure," "from within"
- Structural phrase: "Not X. Y." (two-beat reframe) — e.g. "Felt, not just understood." / "Not driven by thought alone, but aligned with the whole system."
- Avoid: em-dashes (hard rule), AI-speak ("dive deep," "unlock," "journey of self-discovery," "game-changing"), corporate wellness jargon ("wellness," "mindfulness" as a noun, "wellbeing journey," "optimize"), exclamation marks, marketing verbs ("discover," "transform," "unleash," "elevate"), superlatives ("world-class," "cutting-edge"), clichés ("holistic," "synergy"), anything that sounds like an Instagram caption.

## Rhythm
- Sentence length alternates deliberately. A long, exact sentence full of clinical nouns is followed by a short four-to-seven-word line that lands like a breath. Example pair: "Leading well in complexity requires a different kind of skill: the ability to restore congruence in the nervous system, engage emotional resilience, and embody vision for sustainable, grounded, and trustworthy leadership." then "Clarity that comes from congruence, not effort."
- Short fragments are used as stand-alone lines for weight: "It regulates. It slows perception. It brings back what is essential." Three beats, period each.
- Paragraphs are short. Usually one to three sentences. White space does work the copy doesn't have to.
- Lists read like breaths, not bullets. Each item is a complete small phrase with rhythm ("Perception sharpens / Internal noise reduces / Patterns become visible"). No trailing punctuation, parallel grammar.
- Uses colons and commas where most writers would reach for em-dashes. "Structured, yet spacious." not "Structured — yet spacious." The client flagged em-dashes as "too AI"; they are banned.

## Signature Moves
- Short poetic openers that name a feeling or a place, then hand off to explanation. "There comes a point in leadership where more thinking no longer produces more clarity, where the system asks for something other than input." opens the Clean Vision section.
- Three-line reframes in the italic pull-out ("shift-block") style. Parallel structure, each line a move from one state to another: "From thinking to sensing. / From reacting to responding. / From fragmentation to alignment."
- Negative-space lines that describe what isn't there rather than what is: "The desert does not add anything. It removes." / "Nothing here is accidental." / "A rare interruption of speed."
- Named pillars, numbered lowercase roman ("i. Movement and Body Alignment", "ii. Equine Facilitated Learning"). When the copy lists things, it treats each one as a discipline with weight.
- "Not X, but Y" construction to correct a reader's likely assumption: "This is not a peak experience; it is a shift that holds." / "Not by pushing harder, but by regulating the nervous system." / "Felt, not just understood."
- Precise, unfussy factuals in the practical section. Location, duration, group size, price — one line each, no adjectives. The specificity is the reassurance.

## Hard Rules (from CLAUDE.md and brochure convention)
- NO em-dashes (— or &mdash;). Client explicitly flagged them as "too AI." Use commas, colons, semicolons, or parentheses depending on context.
- Refer to "The Care Ranch" custom display font as TCR in any code, commit, or internal context.
- Never filter, recolor, crop, or harmonize client photos. Client's explicit rule.
- No exclamation marks anywhere in body copy.
- No kickers, no orange accent words, no decorative typographic flourishes in copy — the brochure was de-ornamented in v20 ("vision in orange weghalen," "alle oranje headers weg").
- Outcomes are named without superlatives. "Clarity of purpose" not "profound clarity." "Sustainable change" not "life-changing transformation."
- When the brochure uses a Dutch-to-English construction that reads slightly formal (e.g. "Where needed, we are happy to connect briefly to explore alignment"), keep that register. Don't Americanize it.
