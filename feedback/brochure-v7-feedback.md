# Feedback — The Care Ranch Brochure (version 7)

This document contains notes on structure, language, tone and specific wording. No copy has been rewritten — all points are observations and suggestions for consideration.

---

## A note on language

The brochure reads as though it was written with Dutch as the underlying language of thought, and then translated (or composed) into English. This is completely understandable and the result is largely strong. A few phrases carry Dutch sentence structures or word choices that don't quite land in English — these are flagged below not as errors but as places worth a native English speaker's eye before final print.

---

## 1. Structure & Flow

**The "For Who" section appears too late**
Currently it sits after the Method, Experience, Journey *and* the benefits list. Most readers will self-select (or drop off) before they reach it. Consider moving it earlier — ideally before or right after the Method — so the right people recognise themselves quickly.

**Team section placement**
The team bios appear after Practical Information, which makes them feel like an appendix. People and credibility are a major trust-builder for a high-investment experience like this. Consider placing the team earlier — perhaps before or after "The Care Ranch Method".

**The organisational pitch is sandwiched awkwardly**
"Why this matters for organizations" sits between the individual benefits list and the social mission (non-profit) section. It addresses a different buyer (HR / L&D) and breaks the personal narrative. Options: move it to a clearly separated B2B page, or give it its own clearly labelled spread.

**The non-profit section feels abrupt**
The transition from "sustainable personal change" to "we operate as a hybrid model" is quite sudden. The idea is powerful — it could land much better with a brief bridge sentence that connects the personal journey to the broader mission.

---

## 2. Tone & Register

**Two different voices competing**
The opening sections use poetic, sensory language that is very strong:
> *"The land is open. The rhythm is slow. The silence is your peace."*

Later sections shift into corporate/HR language:
> *"From an HR and organizational perspective, this leadership experience supports: Preventive leadership care / Reduced burnout risk..."*

These two registers clash. The corporate bullet-list framing risks undercutting the premium, experiential tone established at the start.

**Inconsistent point of view**
The brochure alternates between first-person ("We develop leadership by...") and third-person ("The Care Ranch is for...") without a clear pattern. Picking one and applying it consistently throughout would tighten the feeling of the document.

**Individual bios vary significantly in tone and length**
- Scott's bio reads as academic/third-person
- Lotte's bio is detailed and reflective, written in first person
- Margreet's bio is brief by comparison

All three would benefit from following the same format. Especially since Margreet is listed as Founder, a slightly fuller bio would feel appropriate.

## 3. Language — likely Dutch-to-English translation points

These are not errors so much as places where the English phrasing carries the logic of Dutch underneath it. Worth a light pass by a native English speaker.

**"FOR WHO"**
In Dutch, *"Voor wie"* is natural and correct. In English, the grammatically expected form is *"For Whom"*. This is a small thing but educated readers will notice it.

**"innovative leadership requires new skills- the ability to restore..."**
Missing a space before the dash. Minor typographic detail.

**"Nothing is excessive or distracts from the transformative journey"**
The verb form here is slightly off in English — *"distracts"* should be *"distracting"* to match the adjective *"excessive"*. A Dutch sentence structure issue.

**"Curious leaders and individuals who sense:"**
The word *"curious"* here translates naturally from Dutch (*nieuwsgierig* / *onderzoekend*) but lands slightly weakly in English for a senior executive audience. Something like *"leaders who sense that something is out of alignment"* is more direct — and is already used effectively elsewhere.

**"A personal leadership experience"** in the opening tagline
The brochure later mentions group sizes of 8–12 participants and notes *"the group creates reflection and perspective."* The word *"personal"* in Dutch carries a broader meaning (individual attention, tailored). In English it implies solo. Worth clarifying — perhaps *"individual"* or *"immersive"* would land more precisely.

---

## 4. Specific Wording Notes

**EMDR disclaimer paragraph**
> *"Where relevant, EMDR may be used as an optional support... without taking a therapeutic focus."*

This reads as a liability caveat rather than brochure copy. If EMDR is part of the offer, it can be described with the same confidence as the other modalities. If a caveat is needed, it may be better placed in pre-intake communication.

**Theory U reference**
> *"The work follows the logic of Theory U"*

This is a niche academic reference (Otto Scharmer / MIT). Most C-suite readers won't know it. Either a brief explanation, or removing the reference entirely — the brochure communicates the same idea in its own language and doesn't need the citation to validate it.

**"EBAS (Elemental Body Alignment System)"**
The technical acronym reads clinical in an otherwise experiential document. Introducing it through Scott's bio (where it belongs to him personally) might feel more natural than leading with the acronym in the method section.

**"Inquire: www.thecareranch.com"**
The word *"Inquire"* as a call to action is passive. Something like *"Apply"*, *"Begin"*, or *"Connect with us"* would be more aligned with the brand's invitation-led positioning.

## 6. Minor Observations

- **No testimonials or social proof** appear in the brochure. Even one brief quote would significantly strengthen credibility for a first-time reader.
- The closing quote — *"A place where heritage is honoured, simplicity restores the nervous system, and leadership returns to congruence"* — is strong but appears after the CTA. Consider whether it belongs before *"Begin your Journey"* rather than after.
- **Pricing** is intentionally not mentioned and directed to the website — this works well for the positioning. The *"by application only"* framing is particularly good.
